<h4>Using historical parasite collections to reconstruct host biology and expand biodiversity records</i></h3>
     <div class="text-secondary"> <i class="fa-solid fa-calendar-days"></i> <em>2024-Present</em></div>
     <p>My current research projects include historical parasitology collections, such as the <b>Lawrence R. Penner (LRP) Parasitology Collection</b> housed at the University of Connecticut. So far, I have transcribed thousands of historical host necropsy cards, organized them into a spreadsheet, and identified hundreds of digenean trematodes and cyclophyllidean cestodes off of slides contained in the collection.</p>

     <li><i class="fa-solid fa-message text-secondary"></i> <b>Horan, S.</b>, Caira, J.N. (2026). Expanding records of North American avian cyclophyllideans. <i>American Society of Parasitologists</i>. Orlando, FL. Poster.</li>

<h4>Environmental factors influencing early-life responses to parasites in cavity-nesting birds</i></h3>
     <div class="text-secondary"> <i class="fa-solid fa-calendar-days"></i> <em>2021-2024</em></div>
     <p>I have contributed to various projects involving the cavity-nesting birds and nest parasite system. Specifically, I have worked with hosts such as the Eastern Bluebird (<i>Sialia sialis</i>) and Tree Swallow (<i>Tachycineta bicolor</i>), and associated nest ectoparasites such as <i>Protocalliphora sialia</i> (blowfly larvae), <i>Ceratophyllus</i> spp. (fleas), and <i>Dermanyssus</i> and <i>Ornithonyssus</i> spp. (mites). Independent projects include...</p>
     <p class="px-3"><b>1.)</b> I artificially manipulated the temperature of the nest boxes of eastern bluebirds. I have been able to determine effects of heat on certain immune gene expression and morphological development of nestlings.</p>
     <p class="px-3"><b>2.)</b> I developed an antigen "vaccine" for <i>Protocalliphora sialia</i> (a parasitic blowfly), and quantified the immune responses to various antigen exposure regimens on Eastern Bluebird nestlings.</p>

     Data were collected through a combination of local fieldwork within the state of Connecticut, and a citizen science project to broaden the dataset to multiple states.

<h4>Potential anuran carriers of emerging salamander pathogen in the pet trade</i></h3>
     <div class="text-secondary"> <i class="fa-solid fa-calendar-days"></i> <em>2020-2021</em></div>
     <p>A potential pathway into North America of devastating salamander fungal pathogen <i>Batrachochytrium salamandrivorans</i> (Bsal) is through the international pet trade. If this emerging pathogen is introduced to America, it could have devastating consequences on salamander populations. This project focused on testing the susceptibility and carrier potential of the most highly traded frog species, pictured below. Data from this project will be used to understand pet trade-related risk of introduction. In collaboration with the Piovia-Scott Lab (Washington State University, Vancouver).</p>

     <p><b>Associated Publications and Presentations:</b>
     <ul><li> <i class="fa-solid fa-file text-secondary"></i> Masecar S., Tavarez-Jimenez, E.J., <b><u>Horan, S.,</u></b>, Draculan, M., Sheley, W.C., McCartney, J.A., Woodhams, D.C., Piovia-Scott, J., Bletz, M.C. (2026). Commonly traded anurans are susceptible to the emerging fungal pathogen  Batrachochytrium salamandrivorans. <i>In Review at Scientific Reports.</i></li>
     

     </ul></p>

<h4>Mitigation of fungal pathogen <i>Batrachochytrium salamandrivorans</i></h3>
     <div class="text-secondary"> <i class="fa-solid fa-calendar-days"></i> <em>2018-2021</em></div>
     <p><i>Batrachochytrium salamandrivorans</i> (Bsal)—a relative of the devastating <i>B. dendrobatidis</i> (Bd)—is a fungal pathogen affecting salamanders that has already caused significant declines in European salamander populations, posing a threat to the North American salamander biodiversity hotspot upon potential introduction.</p>
     <p>Working with Dr. Molly Bletz and the Woodhams Lab at the University of Massachusetts Boston, I've participated in several experiments contributing to our understanding of Bsal infection dynamics, monitoring, and risk assessment.</p>

     <p><b>Associated Publications and Presentations:</b>

     <li><i class="fa-solid fa-message text-secondary"></i> <b>Horan, S.</b> (2020). Environmental factors influencing prevalence of Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis in the eastern United States. <i>Biology Department Honors Senior Thesis Presentation.</i> University of Massachusetts Boston. Presentation.</li>
     <li><i class="fa-solid fa-message text-secondary"></i> <b>Horan, S.,</b> McDonald, C., Bletz, M. (2022). Environmental factors influencing prevalence of <i>Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis</i> in the Eastern Newt (<i>Notophthalmus viridescens</i>). <i>Society for Integrative and Comparative Biology</i>. Presentation.</li>
     
     </ul></p>
